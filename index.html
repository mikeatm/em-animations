Electric Flux vs. Surface Orientation

Rotate the surface and watch how flux Φ = EA·cosθ changes. This is the most-confused concept in the chapter.

0°
3
Key equation:
Φ_E = E · A · cosθ
where θ is the angle between E⃗ and the surface normal n̂.

⚠️ Students confuse θ with the angle between E⃗ and the surface plane. It is the angle with the normal. When the surface is perpendicular to E⃗ (θ=0°), flux is maximum. When parallel to E⃗ (θ=90°), flux is zero.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKE #1
Students often write Φ = EA·sinθ (using the angle to the surface plane instead of the normal). Emphasize: θ is always measured from the normal n̂, not from the surface itself.

Gauss's Law — Sphere Independence

The total flux through any closed surface depends only on the enclosed charge — NOT the radius. Drag the radius slider and see the field and flux values update.

r = 1.0 m
10 nC
Gauss's Law:
∮ E⃗ · dA⃗ = Q_enc / ε₀
The electric field E ∝ 1/r², but the area A ∝ r². These cancel exactly, so the total flux is independent of sphere size.

Notice: The flux (shown in green) stays constant regardless of radius. The field magnitude (blue) changes, but flux doesn't.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKE #2
Students think "bigger Gaussian surface → more flux". Flux measures enclosed charge — not surface size. A sphere of radius 1 m and 100 m around the same charge have identical flux.

Electric Field Lines

Visualise field lines for a single charge, two like charges, or a dipole. Toggle the surface and watch total flux.

Rules to reinforce:
• Field lines start on + charges and end on − charges (or at infinity).
• Lines never cross.
• For the dipole: total flux through a surface enclosing both = 0 (net charge = 0).
• For two +q: a surface enclosing both gives flux = 2q/ε₀.

Charge Distribution on Conductors & Shielding

Shows how charges redistribute on a conductor in an external field and how the interior field vanishes — the basis of Faraday shielding.

3
Key properties at electrostatic equilibrium:
1. E = 0 inside the conductor.
2. Net charge resides on the surface only.
3. E just outside the surface is perpendicular to the surface.
4. Charge density is greatest at points of smallest radius of curvature (sharp tips).

If a charge is placed inside a hollow cavity, an equal but opposite charge appears on the inner surface.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKE #3
Students think "a conductor placed in a field has no charge". The conductor is neutral overall — but charges separate and redistribute. Interior field is zero; surface charges are non-zero.

Electric Potential — Scalar Field Visualization

Hover the canvas to probe the potential at any point. Equipotential lines are shown as concentric circles.

+5 nC
V = kQ/r — potential drops as 1/r (field drops as 1/r²).
Equipotential surfaces are always perpendicular to field lines.
Moving along an equipotential requires zero work.

Reminder: V is a scalar. No direction needed — that's what makes it computationally powerful compared to E⃗.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKE #4
Students confuse V and E — "high potential = strong field" is FALSE. A large uniformly-charged sphere can have high V at the surface but weak E just outside if r is large (E = V/r for sphere). They are related by E = −dV/dr.

Capacitor Charging & Dielectric Effect

Watch a parallel-plate capacitor charge up. Add a dielectric and see C increase, V decrease (for isolated cap) or Q increase (for battery-connected cap).

d = 50
A = 120
κ = 1.0
12 V
C = κε₀A/d — capacitance increases with:
• Larger plate area A (more surface to store charge)
• Smaller separation d (stronger field for same V)
• Higher dielectric constant κ (material polarizes, weakens E, allows more Q for same V)

For battery-connected capacitor with dielectric: V stays constant, Q = CV increases.
For isolated capacitor with dielectric: Q stays constant, V = Q/C decreases.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKE #5
The isolated vs. battery-connected distinction trips up almost every student. The question in the notes (Example 20) is the classic exam question — make sure to drill both scenarios.

Series vs Parallel Resistors — Live Circuit

Adjust resistors and see how current distributes. Kirchhoff's laws shown numerically.

12 V
4 Ω
6 Ω
Series: R_eq = R₁ + R₂ · same current everywhere · voltages add.
Parallel: 1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ · same voltage across each · currents add.

Kirchhoff Voltage Law (loop): Σ ΔV = 0 around any closed loop.
Kirchhoff Current Law (junction): Σ I_in = Σ I_out at any node.
⚠ COMMON MISTAKE #6
Students try to apply the parallel formula to series and vice versa when circuits are mixed. Teach them to always simplify from the innermost sub-circuit outward. Also: R_eq in parallel is always smaller than the smallest individual resistor.

Magnetic Flux Φ_B = BA·cosθ

Rotate a current loop in a uniform magnetic field. See how Φ_B varies with orientation.

0°
3 T
Φ_B = B·A·cosθ where θ is between B⃗ and the area normal n̂.
Units: Weber (Wb) = T·m²

Note the exact parallel with electric flux — same mathematical form, same conceptual traps.
When the loop is perpendicular to B⃗ (θ=0°): maximum flux.
When the loop is parallel to B⃗ (θ=90°): zero flux.
